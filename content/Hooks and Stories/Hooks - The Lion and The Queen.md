The white tiger cub landed on bed of autumn leaves lacking the elegance of her elders. The boy rushing just behind, dressed in tattered pelts, pounced on her back, stomping the animal into the mud. The feline slowly lifted her head from the brown puddle it lay in and let out a long and hoarse moan.

“I win again” the savage boy said. “I am fastest, I am strong, but don’t worry Cici, I will use my strongness to protect our family”. His hands lay on hips, elbows wide-open, imaginary flying cape billowing in the wind.

A voice bent by the weight of years ventured from within the bushes.

“Fast and potent, like a thunder in the night. Its strike relentless, chaotic but precise. But I fear you still have a long way ahead little sapling”.

The sure figure of a bent woman leaning on a cane moved into the clearing. Covered in a dress of decayed leaves, she could have been mistaken for another bush. She smiled an old smile.

The boy precipitously jumped down from his cub sister and rushed to hug the woman.

“Oi, oi, go easy little lightning, I’m an old branch and will crack easily”.

“Can you tell me again the story of queen and the lion?” The boy asked, his eyes unblinking.

The crone’s eyes smiled as she turtled toward a grey levigated boulder. By the time she carefully rested her legs on her elevated stage, the boy was already sitting in front of her, leg crossed gripping his own feet and furiously rocking back and forth.

“You always ask for the same, would you not prefer to hear the one on the red hopper-bird?” The woman said, the smile now sculpted on the deep ridges on her face.

“No, the queen and the lion please” The boy said, now serious in his demands. And she began.

“Once upon a time, in a land tucked in a dream, there lived a King and his Queen.

He was burdened but stood straight and she tended to her people night and day.

Times were difficult and her king had to leave, to travel beyond sea, he had prosperity to achieve.

The Queen waited his return, but he would not come, for endless time and more she waited, and she wept alone.

Devoured by her longing, her heart would not recover, so she chose love for one over the responsibility of a mother.

She abandoned her people to travel the sea, but to find her King there was no guarantee.

One hundred days and one hundred nights went by with her eyes scanning, she persevered storms, cold, and famine.

Dolphins, whales and sharks followed her spark, to witness whether she’d find her light in the dark.

So determined she was to locate her lord, she even merited the attention of a God.

His eyes watched, his divine marvelled, at the lost flea in a puddle.

One hundred days and one hundred nights was her travel, when a voice broke the silence of her struggle.

“Little fish” he asked from the depth, “What do you desire?” and she knew what he meant.

With no answer needed, she leaned from her boat, and looked at one that from underwater spoke.

Full moonlight gave shape to the creature and its power, a blue leonine head on a bestial body large as a tower.

“And what will you give?” The God said and its voice soared, its growls so mighty the night sky gored.

The Queen wanted to see him better, but her feet slipped on the deck, her world with abyss merged.

The icy ocean numbed her pain under its cover, its vast silence hugged her like a nursing mother.

The shadow encircled her again while she lived, and again asked “Little fish, what will you give?”.

Once more answer she did not. At peace, she opened her mouth and let her lungs fill with God’s salt and storm.

She then travelled again far, far away. Fields in bloom. The smell of lilies, sun on her face.

She turned to see her king eying his Queen. The man of which she had long dreamed.

The king hinted a smile and crooked his head, with aching heart he hugged and kissed his Queen nonetheless.

And the Queen was happy. And the Queen was at peace. She found her King in the fields of the [Water Lion](Actors/Water%20Lion.md)’s dream.

The narrator stopped and looked at the cubs, the boy and the tiger now lay in identical position with their belly to the ground, tense on their elbows.

“That’s not how it ends, gran” The boy said, shaking his head energetically.

“Yes my boy, I always forget this part” The woman replied.

“And the water Lion took from her what was now his, carried it at the bottom, hid in an emerald stone beneath the seas

The light of the stone would forever shine within the darkness, for no-one could rival the strength and beaty of her desire and its unrelenting prowess.”
