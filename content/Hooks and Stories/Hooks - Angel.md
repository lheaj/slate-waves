The doctor eyed her reflection on the stained window glass. The left corner of her lip arched in disgust. She spoke without moving her gaze.

“Don’t move, or your left lung will collapse again. We have done our best to mechanize it, but the diaphragm pumps are not settled yet and if you turn too much they will fail.”

“Thank you.”

The voice behind her belonged to frail figure. The girl was covered with a thin white sheet from waist down, leaving her wounded chest bare. On her right side, bloodless paper-thin skin covered her ribcage. On the left, a large metal plaque blanketed her torso from shoulder down to the hip, guarding the remnants of her human heart.

“I need to thank you.”

Her voice struggled to rise above the steady beat of her respirator. She persevered.

“I died, didn’t I? You saved me. I remember your hands, your voice.”

“We healed you yes. And no, you did not die, otherwise we would not be speaking now. Ari found you down at the port. You have to thank her. She often visits the ones that lack a home, she carries food and poetry. She says it gives her purpose, often bringing back a smile. But this time she brought back a dying girl with rust in both lobes on her left lung. Your Bronchiolis were shattered, and the Pleura had already ironed. The sickness had spread through your blood. I’m sorry I had to replace your neck and part of your jaw too. I failed to save those.”

The bits of truth in that last sentence soured in her mouth with the taste of gastric acid.

“I will leave you to rest now, be still”.

The girl eyes followed the small stature of her saviour walking out of the room, as soon as the door closed, she let go of the effort pulled together to thank her hero and plunged into a deep sleep.

[Dr. Elen Voss](Actors/Dr.%20Elen%20Voss.md) twisted the lock slowly as to not disturb her patient. She leaned over the wood with her forehead and squinted her eyes pushing another horrible thought down her throat.

She walked steadily and apathetically, nodding to colleagues and patients. Her feet moved down the resonating hallways rhythmically to the memory of a thousand trips to the morgue. Once alone within the damp stone walls of the lowest level of the hospital, she removed a small rock from a wall revealing a keyhole made for one key only.

Inside, her hand flicked a gilded switch on the side, its cables warped within a brass tube anchored to the stone, running to the ceiling, connected to single elliptical vial filled with a translucent liquid. As the lighting touched the substance, it radiated a faint but persistent green glow, pushing the shadows of each object back to the four walls. Voss proceeded steadily among the operating tables littered with flasks and alchemical tubes running live experiments.

Her steps weakened as she passed by the two cylindrical tanks housing the girl’s stolen organs.

She halted, her sullen look fixated on the stone pavement. In a rush she reached for a familiar drawer and drew a surgical knife, its glistening silver reflecting the green light onto her desperate expression. She pressed it to her own throat. Icy, it drew a red tear from her scarred skin, she placed it always on the same spot. Her hands trembled for an infinite moment.

She screamed silent as the weapon slipped through her finger, its contact with the solid pavement muffled by the bubbling of the preservation tanks.

“I cannot do it anymore” She said, now sitting on her knees, almost talking to the knife.

“I am always so close and yet so far. It is always one expendable sacrifice to find a cure for an entire civilization. Yet I fail, and I fail, and I fail.”

However, listless and hollow, she rose, approached her steel-framed table, and as she worked. She never looked at girl’s stolen lung breathing in its electrified formaldehyde solution.
