
A trickle of sweat fell from the olive smooth chin of [Garabatta](Actors/Garabatta.md) down into his neatly ironed white shirt.  The jabot on the front felt out of place on his person, but this was a special day, and it demanded the best he could afford. There was no halting the tall goblin as he filtered through a colander of workers and merchants. How could he be so late?

He had been pacing through his room for hours, all night, pondering over four or five words to declare a sentiment so long held and so dear to him it flet impossible to let it escape, and even harder to accompany with the right medium. Every form and manner seemed juvenile and trivial when compared to her radiance. Lost in the failed preparations of his speech, he had ignored the light filtering through a stained and glossy window, trapped in a rhythmical pace through the few feet of dusty wooden floor separating one wall to the other.

Now, chatter mixed with violent haggling encircled his ears muffling the anxiety in his heart as he eyed through swarms of buyers and sellers for the right way. There it was, the clock shop at the corner of Ret Road. It marked the beginning of a long descent toward the port. Without hesitation Garabatta bolted the corner, his legs moving quicker than his mind allowed. As he turned, he bumped into an old man, sweeping the cane out of his feet and propelling the elderly figure fast to the ground. Already, Garabatta was seeing within his mind what he should have, and normally would have, done. Kneel next to the gentleman, apologize profusely while removing his hat in respect, and bow in remorse. He might have even donated coins to compensate or pay for any medical expense. But not today. There was simply no time. He grimaced, frowning with regret as he rushed the cobblestones downhill.

If there was one upside to being late, it was that he didn't have any time left to second-guess how he would have spoken to her. He held his top hat in a sweaty punch, as he snuck under a cargo of fresh fish, jumped a mirror being carefully carried on a collector’s exposé and slid on the wet floor in front of [Smallport](Smallport.md)’ favourite pastry shop, startling the maid going about her morning cleaning.

“So-Sorry!” He waved apologetic to her direction, without stopping.

It was all a rush, and as much as he hated his tardiness and indecisiveness, Garabatta was relieved to be free of thoughts for a few minutes. Maybe he would be too late. As horrible as it sounded, that would have given him an excuse to avoid confronting his truly greatest fear.

A fresh breeze caressed his cheeks cooling the sweat from his brow, as he quested the port for slot number eight. To his delight, and dread, the passengers were still boarding. It didn’t take longer than a second to see [Patricia](Actors/Patricia.md). Her short stature stood out from the cascade of man and women queuing orderly. She had a black chemise with a white collar, beige trousers and tight-height laced black boots. Her curly hair was tied roughly in a blonde blob at the top of her head, leaving her halfling ears exposed. She was waiting still in front of the ship, gesturing to a young family to step ahead of her as she tightened the straps of her large backpack while glancing searchingly with her crystal blue eyes left and right.

At the sight of him, she smiled.

Patricia stood up, straightened her back and rested an elbow on her baggage standing as tall as her.

“For a moment I thought you would not come to see me off!”

“Sorry, I was… I was reviewing the early markets rates for brass strings and I just lost track of time. You know how it is… Those rates must be reviewed every morning before the open.”

Garabatta forced a smile while grappling with his hat with both hands, wriggling its contour maniacally.

Patricia’s shoulder relaxed as the sky within her eyes moved from the goblin to the ship.

“I wouldn’t dare distract you from your trades. But I’m glad you made it Garby. I would have hated to leave without saying goodbye.”

She was silent for a while after that. Garabatta knew it was his time to speak. But as every second piled onto the previous, the lack of words seemed to make it harder and harder for him to say what he wanted to say. So, he resorted to something else:

“You-you don’t have to go, you know. My textile business is growing rapidly, and I have a mind to invest in a book collector’s auction as well. I am sure you could find employment there and learn a lot from those bo…”

“You know this wouldn’t work” She cut him sharply, still holding onto her own smile.

“You know this book auction business you just came up with has no financial standing. I don’t have to tell you that Garby, nobody understands this stuff better than you. Besides, I have already accepted Borbor’s offer to set up post in Watersafe. They say the city is a marvel of technology and art. That all the luminaries of our time are visiting its glass halls to exchange philosophies and debate to time’s end. That’s my place Garby, I need to go, and I will be forever thankful to the baldie for his offer.”

If a beaten dog’s expression would be transferable to a face, that would be the best description of how the goblin looked then. His hands stilled along with his heart, eyes falling hopelessly to stare the ground. That was it. She had made up her mind and nothing he could have said would change that. He knew her well enough to have understood this would be the outcome long before she spoke her mind, no matter what grand demonstration or promise he could have come up with. But reality is sometimes bleaker when presented then when imagined, as in that, there is no subconscious hope to help.

“Hey”

She was so close to him he could smell the sweet paper and dry ink that always accompanied her. Her soft hand pillowed under the goblin’s chin and lifted his look to meet hers.

“I’ll come back someday, and then we’ll have so much to talk about! You’ll be a rich merchant, the richest of all town, and I’ll have learnt all there is to learn and then more.”

He lacked the strength to pretend much further as his lips quivered.

“Hold onto this for me”

She presented a rolled-up tie. Red and lucid. Roped up with a green bow in the fashion of a present.

“The Perisians say red is a lucky colour, the colour of earth where nature is most present. I bought this long ago and always thought it would fit you well.”

She eyed inquisitive his pompous jabot.

“Maybe on a different shirt, this one isn’t really you, Garby.”

Her lips brushed his cheek, they were cold on his flushed skin still warm from the run, freezing him on the spot.

Patricia shouldered her backpack, not without effort, and walked up the gangplank. As if possessed by someone else, someone braver and decisive, Garabatta ran close to the plank as she climbed her way up.

“I’ll do it!” He yelled.

“I promise! I will be the richest in all Smallport! And I will buy so much and prepare, and I will wait, and I will be ready for when you are back! I will wait Patricia!”

The young halfling stood a moment, blocking the queue of travellers just behind. Then she turned her bust and smiled again.

“I know you will Garby.”
