"Little one..." That's how Zhuazhua, now in his late nineties, always began his cautionary tales to his now-adult granddaughter.

"There is something intrinsically wrong about magic—the ability to change nature and bend it to one's will. One could say magic is an exogenous factor within the fabric of the universe. It disrupts a carefully engineered balance, bringing power to some and tragedy to others."

He continued, adjusting his askew pair of silvery glasses on his crooked, shrivelled nose.

"The truth, of course, is much more complex. Magic isn't a free-for-all pass to be used or misused without price or consequence. Some pay dearly for their dangerous reliance on frivolous spells and gross words devoid of noble goals. Only those aggressively driven truly find magic. The pursuit seldom leaves room for anyone but the very best or the very worst hearts."

Zhuazhua paused, looking out the window, lost in thought. His granddaughter never bothered him or pointed out when his dementia kicked in. She loved hearing the old man tell his tales.

"Yes, yes, indeed..." He continued.

"Stories tell of one such mad beings, a persona larger than life, so charismatic they could charm entire cities with their talk, filled with real knowledge and exciting bubbliness. This mage, as we shall call them, was one of the few who possessed real power. And I don't mean magic missiles or the odd -polymorphing your repulsive mother-in-law into a turtle so she might finally stop picking at you- No, I mean magic that can create, destroy, and bend life, space, and time.

Smallport, a city of technology and commerce, has never had much culture or treasure in the arcane arts and has never seen such an extravaganza of power within its small confines. So, you bet that when the mage came to visit the little growing alcove of wanna-be-famous people, they all came to freely drool over their words and bask in the mage's excellence, hoping to absorb some of that sinister intellect that made the mysterious being so powerful.

All the merchants of the town came offering gifts and asking for favours. Some gave chests of gold or offered the most advanced weaponry. Others brought the gift of servitude and manual labour. You see, some of that manual labour consisted of unskilled commoners looking for a way up or trying to make ends meet to feed their families or friends. And, you see, when you put commoners and goons in charge of carrying powerful artifacts from one of the last remaining wielders of world-bending, will-instigated sorcery, you often end up with spectacular city-wide explosions and irreversible meddling with the fabric of time and the multiverse."

Zhuazhua paused again, scratching his stubby white beard.

"But well, that's a story for someone else to tell. Darling. Why did I tell you this again? I feel we had this little chat already, didn’t we?"
